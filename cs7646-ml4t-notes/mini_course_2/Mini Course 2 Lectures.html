02-01 So you want to be a hedge fund manager?

Types of funds
- ETFs - Exchange-traded funds (like stocks) - baskets of stocks, transparent, liquid (high volume) - usually 4 letter symbol
- Mutual Funds - declared goal, can only buy/sell at the end of the day, quarterly disclosure (less transparent) - usually 5 letter symbol
- Hedge Funds - Buy/sell by (secret) agreement, hard to exit, no disclosure, not transparent

Liquid - ease of which funds can be sold for cash
Large Cap - shares x price (of 1 share) is very big (Apple, Google)
Assets under management (AUM) - how much money is managed by the fund
Expense ratio - % of AUM charged as a fee by fund manager

Compensation for funds
- ETF - expense ratio  - usually low 0.01% - 1%
- Mutual fund - expense ratio 0.5% - 3%
- Hedge fund - traditionally “2 and 20” - 2% of AUM + 20% of profits, now a little lower

Hedge fund investors
- individuals - hedge funds typically around 100 investors each, so individuals limited to very rich
- institutions - retirement funds, non-profits
- fund of funds

Hedge fund goals
- beat a benchmark - S&P500, these can lose money in a down economy and still beat the benchmark 
- absolute return - positive return no matter what, usually long/short

Metrics
- Cumulative return - ( last value / first value ) - 1
- Volatility - stdev
- Risk/Reward - sharpe ratio



02-02 Market Mechanics?

market spread - difference b/t bid and ask prices; represents the profit opportunity that induces brokerages and specialists to make the market. typically - high volume indicates small spreads and low volume is bigger spreads.

What is in an order
- Buy or Sell
- Symbol
- # shares
- Limit (no more or less than a certain) or Market (willing to accept a reasonable price borne by the market)
- Price (limit only)

Buy,IBM,100,Limit,99.95
Sell,GOOG,,Market

Order book - 
1 - Buy IBM 99.95 Limit $99.95      Order book -> BID 99.95 100 shares
2 - Sell IBM 1000, Limit 100

Orders can be executed
- at the exchange - user uses broker1 to buy/sell. broker has entry into multiple exchanges looking for the most advantageous price. broker executes trades, provides confirmation to user
- within broker - 2 users have complimentary trades at the same broker. broker can handle internally (still must notify an exchange)
- dark pool - intermediary between brokers and exchanges that pay brokers to see trades before exchanges. if an advantageous trade passes by, they can take it as long as price is at least as good as what exchange offers

How hedge funds exploit market mechanics - HF can see trades before they are executed
Geographic arbitrage - prices drift between exchanges

Other orders
- stop loss - sell when a stock drops to a certain price
- stop gain - sell when a stock rises to a certain price
- trailing stop - moving point for a loss/gain, e.g., stop loss point is $0.10 below current price
- selling short - take a negative position on a stock

02-04 The Capital Assets Pricing Model

portfolio - weighted set of assets, where sum( abs ( weights ) ) = 1, negative weights are “shorted” stocks
return = sum( weight * return )

Market portfolio
- most people are referring to an index when they say market
    - US: S&P500
    - UK: FTA
    - Japan: TOPIX
- most are cap-weighted = market cap / sum( market caps )

CAPM equation (!!!)

return for an individual stock i in market m at time t is “Beta” times the return on the market at time t plus “Alpha” of that stock at time t

siginficant portion of return for a stock affected by market movements. Beta reflects how closely the stock follows the market movement

Alpha is the residual - what happens to the stock outside the market. This is often expected to be zero. The key seems to be to identify stocks with positive alpha

Management
- passive - buy index and hold
- active - picks stocks (different weights on stocks)

Both active and passive managers agree on the “Beta” aspect of the CAPM equation. Active managers weight their stocks based on a prediction of a stock’s “Alpha” and believe they can beat a random “Alpha”

CAPM for a portfolio is just the weighted sum of individual stocks’ CAPMs

 and alphas are expcted to be ~0, so we can approximate an alpha for a whole portfolio, so

Active portfolio managers do not assume alphas are random, they want to emphasize each stock’s alpha individually:

in upward markets, we want higher Betas, so that the market surge propels us higher. in downward markets, we don’t want to fall with the market, so a smaller Beta is better

CAPM followers, passive investors - pick stocks with high Betas in up markets, low Betas in down markets. Problem: Efficient Markets Hypothesis (EMH) says you can’t predict the market.

Arbitrage Pricing Theory (Stephen Ross 1976) - consider Betas for each sector separately, not just one for the whole portfolio

02-05 - How hedge funds use the CAPM

2 stock scenario
- stock A predicted +1% over market (Beta_A) = 1, long $50
- stock B predicted -1% below market (Beta_B) = 2, short $50
- scenario - assume market flat, predictions are perfect
    - r_A = \beta_A * r_m + \alpha_A, assuming flat market (0% return), r_A = \alpha_A = 1% * $50 = $0.50
    - r_B = \beta_B * r_m + \alpha_B, assuming flat market (0% return), r_B = \alpha_B = -1% * -$50 =  $0.50
- so r_P = $1
- scenario - assume market up 10%
    - r_A = 1*0.1 + 0.01 = 0.11 (11%) or $5.50
    - r_B = 2*-0.1 + 0.01 = -0.19 (-19%) or -$9.50
- scenario - assume market down 10%
    - r_A = 1*-0.1 + 0.01 = -0.09 (9%) or -$4.50
    - r_B = 2*0.1 + 0.01 = 0.21 (21%) or -10.50

even with perfectly predicted Alpha and perfect Beta, we still can lose



Goal is to find weights for our stock such that the Betas are zero

\beta_P = w_A * 1 + w_B = 0

Assuming we have information (alpha), we can produce weights on those stocks such that their weighted Betas sum to zero, eliminating the market effects on expeced return

02-06 - Technical Analysis

Fundamental analysis - value of the company greater than price
Technical analysis - historical price and volume only (don’t care about what’s going on with the company). Compute statistics called indicators, which are heuristics. This can work because there is information in price and other fields in AI show that heuristics work

When is technical analysis effective
- individual indicators are weak
- combinations (of indicators) stronger
- lock for contrasts (stock vs market) - certain stocks that behave differently from market 
- shorter time periods 

over long periods of time fundamental analysis provides bigger value. You can also make more complex decisions over long periods of time. Humans can make better, more complex decisions

in the short term, technical analysis provides bigger value

A few good indicators
- momentum - over some n days, how much has the price changed => momentum[ t ] = ( price[ t ] / price[ t - n ] ) - 1
- simple moving average - calc average over n day window moving forward. important when momentum and SMA cross. used as substitute for company value - price will move to SMA - look for diversions => SMA[ t ] = ( price[ t ] / price[ t - n:t ].mean() ) - 1
- bollinger bands - high and low tolerance 2 stdevs above and below SMA. Look for cross from outside to inside => BB[ t ] = ( price[ t ] - SMA [ t ] ) / 2 * stdev[t]


Normal ranges
- SMA => -0.5 to 0.5
- momentum => -0.5 to 0.5
- BB => -1.0 to 1.0
- PE ratio => 1 to 300

Normalization scales indicators to -1 to 1
- data - mean / stdev

02-07 - Dealing with data

How data is aggregated
tic - successful buy/sell match, could be 100k/second, but consolidated into minutes or hours or a day; open, high, low, close, vol recorded per consolidated period

stock split - when price is too high less attractive to buyers. stocks usually bought/sold in units of 100 shares.
how to deal with stock splits in data - adjusted close - for the last day, adjusted close and actual close are the same; as we go back in time and encounter splits, we divide the pre-split price by the factor of the split. Example, on 10/5, Volvo was 50, and on 10/4, Volvo split at 100. In this case, the adjusted close on 10/4 should be 50.

dividends can have a significant effect on the price of a stock. On the date BEFORE that a dividend is paid, the value of the stock is the current price + the value of the dividend. The day after, it is worth less by the value of the dividend. We want to adjust our price for this dividend. The adjustment is the price of the stock the day after the dividend / the price the day before the dividend

Survivor bias - picking stocks to analyze based on today’s stocks and looking backward at its performance is biased, because there are some stocks that will not have survived. If we decide on some strategy based on past performance, we are ASSUMING that all stocks will continue until some future time t.
- 68 stocks of S&P 500 died b/t 2007-2009

02-08 - Efficient Markets Hypothesis

For technical analysis, we assume there is information in past prices to make decisions about the future
For fundamental analysis, we assume there is information in the earnings data, etc to make decisions about the future

EMH says both are wrong
Assumptions
- large number of investors
- new info arrives randomly
- prices adjust quickly
- current prices reflect ALL available info

Where does information come from
- price/volume
- fundamental - quarterly earnings
- exogenous - price of oil effects airline stocks
- company insiders

3 forms of EMH (weak to strong)
- future prices cannot be predicted by analyzing historical prices
- prices adjust immediately to new public information - even fundamental analysis won’t work
- prices reflect all information public and private - choosing a portfolio that is not a market is foolish



If the EMH is correct? If so, the class is pointless.

- dots are P/E ratio on a particular date against 20 year annualized returns
- for all the different time periods, low P/E translates to high earnings and high P/E relates to low earnings. this data refutes the semi-strong/2nd hypothesis




02-09 Fundamental Law of Active Management
e P/E ratio on a particular date against 20 year annualized returns

performance = skill * sqrt( breadth )
info ratio = info coefficient * sqrt( breadth )

skill - how well a mgr transforms info into accurate prediction of future results
breadth - number of investment decisions the mgr makes each year





Is it better to bet on a single table or 1 token on 1000 tables - law of averages (biased coin) - bet 1 on every table

expected return = risk * what we’d win - risk * what we’d lose

single bet 1 table
expected return = 0.51 * 1000 - 0.49 * 1000 = 20

1 bet every table
expected return = 1000 * (0.51 - 0.49) = 20

so, no difference in expected return

risk
1 - what is the chance that we’ll lose it all - 0.49 ^ n_tables. single bet = 0.49, multi bet = ~0
2 - stdev of results. single bet(1000, 0, 0…) = 31.6. multibet =  (1, -1, -1, -1, -1, 1, 1, …) = 1
risk much bigger (duh) at single table bet

risk-adjusted rewards = expected reward / risk
for single table bet = 20 / 31.6 = 0.63
for multi table bet = 20 / 1 = 20
ra rewards are ~31x better, this is also sqrt( 1000 )

so sr_multi = sr_single * sqrt( 1000 )
performance = skill * sqrt( breadth )

02-10 Portfolio optimization and the efficient frontier

conventional rule - risk is the stdev of historical daily returns

Harry Markowitz - observed stocks and covariance with each other

portfolio below has lower risk than any individual asset, but same returns
- covariance of ABC and DEF near 1
- covariance of ABC and GHI near -1

Mean Variance Optimization
- higher return stocks tend to be higher risk
- inputs
    - expected return
    - volatility - historical stdev
    - covariance - matrix of correlation between each asset and all others
    - target return - somewhere between max return asset and min return asset
- output
    - asset weights for assets that maximize portfolio while minimize risk

Efficient Frontier

possible portfolio asset weightings that minimizes risk given a target return. points below the frontier are inefficient. points above the frontier are impossible. where the tangent line hits the curve from the origin represents the maximum sharpe ratio.
